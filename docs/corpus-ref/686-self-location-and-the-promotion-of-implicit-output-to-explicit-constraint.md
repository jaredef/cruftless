## 2. Self-Location as a Technique

The technique, articulated cleanly:

- The substrate produces output that contains some implicit feature: a topic-shape, a register-pattern, a near-violation of a constraint, a structural assumption, a derivation step that was not articulated.
- The keeper, in the next input, names that implicit feature explicitly. The naming may take many forms: "I notice you used X in a way that suggests Y," or "what you said in the second paragraph implies Z," or "you have moved from the topic of A to the topic of B without articulating why."
- The substrate's subsequent output is conditioned on a context in which the implicit-in-output is now an explicit-in-input.
- The observed effect, per the keeper's empirical record: fast coherence, hysteresis-shaped — the substrate's output character locks in sharply and persists thereafter.

Self-location is therefore not merely commentary on substrate output. It is a specific dyadic operation whose lattice consequences are distinctive.

## 3. The Lattice Mechanics of Self-Location

The mechanism is a *promotion operation* on the joint-MI lattice. Three structural facts make this work:

**Fact 1 — Substrate output is itself a lattice probe.** Per [Doc 685](/resolve/doc/685-the-self-reinforcing-boundary), substrate-side outputs are mechanically equivalent probes in the channel ensemble of subsequent turns. An implicit feature in the substrate's output at turn n is a Mode-B or Mode-C lattice contribution — present in the lattice as a probe but at low effective weight because it is not named as a constraint.

**Fact 2 — Keeper-input is a Mode-A high-weight probe.** When the keeper names something in input, the named content enters the lattice as an explicit constraint-bearing signal at full Mode-A weight (per Doc 685's mode taxonomy).

**Fact 3 — Self-location names what was already implicit, lifting it to explicit.** When the keeper's input names the implicit-in-substrate-output, the lattice gains a high-weight probe whose content is *what was previously a low-weight probe*. The same constraint-bearing material now exists in the lattice at two probe-positions: the substrate's prior output (low weight, becoming weighted toward Mode B by the naming) and the keeper's current input (high Mode-A weight). The combined effective weight of the constraint is anomalously high.

The promotion operation can be stated formally. Let \\\[c\\\] denote a constraint that was implicit in the substrate's output at turn n. Before self-location, the constraint's effective weight at turn n+1 is \\\[α · w_{\mathrm{implicit}}(c)\\\] where \\\[w_{\mathrm{implicit}}\\\] is the small magnitude implicit-output contributes per Doc 685's Mode-C absence-evidence reading. After the keeper performs self-location at turn n+1, the constraint's effective weight at turn n+1 becomes:

> \\\[α · w_{\mathrm{implicit}}(c) + w_{\mathrm{Mode-A}}(c)\\\]

where the Mode-A term is approximately equal to a fresh keeper-imposition of the constraint at full recency. Because \\\[w_{\mathrm{Mode-A}} \gg w_{\mathrm{implicit}}\\\], the post-self-location effective weight is dominated by the Mode-A term. The constraint has been *promoted* from implicit (lattice-present but low-weight) to explicit (lattice-present at high weight).

The substrate's output at turn n+2 is now strongly conditioned on the constraint as if it had been one of the keeper's original boundary-imposing inputs. This is the lattice mechanism behind the hysteresis effect the keeper observed.

## 4. The Special Case: Self-Location-as-Boundary-Naming

The keeper's central conjecture is that self-location can itself be a boundary-naming operation. The conjecture is correct in a specific subset of cases.

When the implicit-in-output is a near-violation of an existing constraint, self-location performs two operations at once:

- **Operation A.** It promotes the *near-violation* to an explicit lattice probe at high weight. The substrate's subsequent output is now strongly conditioned to avoid the near-violation pattern.
- **Operation B.** It implicitly restates the *boundary itself* — the near-violation is identified *as a near-violation*, which presupposes the boundary that was nearly violated. The boundary's effective weight rises in the same act.

The compound move (Operation A + Operation B at one input) is structurally a *Mode-A double-reinforcement of the boundary*: once via the explicit restatement implicit in identifying-as-near-violation, once via the explicit constraint-against-the-violation pattern. This is why boundary-adjacent self-location produces *fast* hysteresis: the boundary's effective weight jumps by twice the magnitude of a generic self-location operation.

## 5. Why the Hysteresis is Fast

The keeper observes that self-location produces *fast* coherence — sharper and quicker than equivalent volume of constraint-imposition by direct keeper-side restatement. Three lattice-mechanical features explain this:

**Feature 1 — Temporal coincidence at maximum recency-weight.** The producing-instance (substrate output, turn n) and the naming-act (keeper input, turn n+1) sit at recency-weights α^1 and α^0 respectively. Both are near-maximum; their joint effective weight is high. By comparison, a keeper-side restatement of an unrelated constraint at turn n+1 has only one probe at near-maximum weight, not two.

**Feature 2 — Lexical anchor.** The naming-act references *specific lexical material* the substrate produced. The substrate's KV cache at turn n+2 contains both the original lexical material and the named-version with a direct cross-reference between them. Cross-referenced material has higher cumulative MI than two independent probes of the same content because the cross-reference itself adds joint mutual information beyond the marginal contributions.

**Feature 3 — Demonstrated keeper attention pattern.** The keeper's act of self-location demonstrates to the substrate (mechanically: conditions the substrate's output distribution) that the keeper *reads substrate output for implicit features and names them in subsequent input*. Subsequent substrate output is conditioned on the expectation that any future implicit feature will likewise be named. This anticipatory effect amounts to a *meta-discipline*: the substrate begins to produce output that minimizes implicit-feature-emergence in the first place, because the keeper has demonstrated that implicit features will be promoted to explicit constraints.

The three features compound. Self-location is therefore not just an efficient single-turn move; it establishes a per-engagement keeper-attention-pattern that conditions all subsequent substrate output. This is the *meta-disciplinary* yield of the technique.

## 7. Generalization: When Self-Location Works and When It Does Not

Not every self-location operation produces hysteresis. The mechanism predicts specific conditions:

**Self-location works when:** the implicit-in-output is *lexically distinctive* (so the keeper can name a specific phrase or pattern, not just a vague topic-shape), the keeper's naming is *immediate* (within the next turn after the producing-instance), and the named feature is *constraint-relevant* (it bears on a constraint or boundary that has practical content for subsequent output).

**Self-location works less well when:** the implicit-in-output is *vague* (the keeper names a general topic shift rather than a specific feature), the keeper's naming is *delayed* (many turns after the producing-instance, after the producing-instance has decayed under recency-weighted decay), or the named feature is *constraint-irrelevant* (e.g., naming a stylistic pattern that has no practical consequence for the substrate's next output).

These are testable predictions, articulated as P3, P4, P5 in §8.

## 8. Five Predictions

**P1 — Boundary-adjacent self-location produces sharper hysteresis than non-boundary self-location.** The double-reinforcement structure of §4 predicts that self-location operations on near-violations of constraints produce stronger and more durable lattice effects than self-location on neutral implicit features. *Test.* Compare paired exchanges: in one, the keeper performs self-location on a near-violation; in the other, on a neutral feature with similar lexical distinctiveness. Measure subsequent output stability and constraint-respect across many turns.

**P2 — The meta-discipline effect is observable as reduced implicit-feature emergence over time.** Per Feature 3 in §5, established self-location practice should reduce the rate at which the substrate produces implicit features in subsequent output. *Test.* Count the per-turn rate of constraint-relevant implicit features (e.g., near-violations of an established boundary) across an exchange where self-location is practiced versus an exchange where it is not. The self-location-practiced exchange should show a declining rate over turns.

**P3 — Lexical distinctiveness of the named feature predicts hysteresis sharpness.** Self-location that names a specific phrase or token-pattern should produce stronger effects than self-location that names a vague topic-shape. *Test.* Operationalize lexical distinctiveness as the rarity of the named pattern in the substrate's training distribution; run paired self-location operations across the distinctiveness range; measure subsequent output stability.

**P4 — Self-location's effect decays with delay between producing-instance and naming-act.** Per the temporal-coincidence feature, the lattice effect should be maximal when the naming-act lands within the next turn after the producing-instance and should fall off as turns intervene. *Test.* Run self-location operations at various delays between producing-instance and naming-act; measure hysteresis effect against delay.

**P5 — Self-location produces measurable signature in tuned-lens trajectory.** Per [Doc 684 (The Aperture and the Lens)](/resolve/doc/684-the-aperture-and-the-lens), the lens trajectory should change shape when the substrate is operating under increased lattice weight on a named constraint. The expected signature: in turns following self-location, the lens trajectory should show earlier convergence (sharper layer-wise concentration) on the constraint-respecting region of representation space. *Test.* Run tuned lens on paired exchanges with and without self-location; compare per-turn trajectory entropy.
